# Analyzing-Risk-Factors-for-Coronary-Heart-Disease-
Analyzing Risk Factors for Coronary Heart Disease 

Project Overview

The Framingham Heart Study was a landmark research project that began in 1948. This study was one of the first to follow a large group of healthy people over many years to see who developed heart disease and what they had in common, giving us the concept of a “risk factor.”. We analyzed the Framingham Heart Study dataset to identify major risk factors associated with coronary heart disease (CHD) using statistical inference and predictive modeling.

Research Question

What are the most significant risk factors for developing coronary heart disease, and can we build a model to predict a person’s risk based on their health profile?

Dataset:
The Framingham Heart Study dataset contains 11,627 patient observations and 39 demographic, behavioral, and clinical variables related to cardiovascular health.

Methods:
Data cleaning
Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)
t-tests
Chi-square tests
Linear Probability Models
Multiple regression
Results

Key Findings:
1. Systolic blood pressure was the strongest predictor of coronary heart disease (CHD).
Among the variables analyzed, systolic blood pressure had the largest impact in the linear probability model, suggesting that elevated blood pressure is strongly associated with increased CHD risk.
2. Total cholesterol was significantly associated with CHD.
Patients with CHD had average cholesterol levels approximately 11.22 mg/dL higher than patients without CHD, indicating a meaningful relationship between cholesterol and disease risk.
3. Diabetes showed the strongest categorical relationship with CHD.
Exploratory analysis found that diabetes produced the largest proportional difference between patients with and without CHD, making it one of the most important risk factors in the dataset.
